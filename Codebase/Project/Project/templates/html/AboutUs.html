PermaFrost Effects

What is Permafrost?

Permafrost is ground (soil, rock and ice) that stays frozen for at least two years.

  • Found mainly in Arctic and Sub-Arctic regions
  • Depth can range from a few centimetres to hundreds of metres
  • Contains soil, rock, ice, and ancient organic carbon
  • Acts like a natural foundation that supports land and buildings
  • The top “active layer” thaws in summer and refreezes in winter

What We Can Do

  • Use satellite data (e.g., InSAR) to detect early ground movement
  • Map risk zones near infrastructure and communities
  • Use insulation mats, elevated foundations, and thermosyphons
  • Improve drainage to stop warm water from soaking the soil
  • Work with communities to plan long-term adaptation strategies

Why is it Important?

Permafrost stabilizes terrain and stores greenhouse gases that would otherwise be released into the atmosphere.

  • Supports roads, homes, runways, and pipelines in the North
  • Prevents ground collapse and erosion
  • Holds huge amounts of carbon and methane
  • Controls drainage patterns and wetland ecosystems
  • Helps protect coastlines by keeping frozen bluffs stable

What Causes it to Melt?

Warming temperatures and land disturbance speed up permafrost thaw.

  • The Arctic is warming about four times faster than the rest of the planet
  • Less snow and ice exposes darker surfaces that absorb more heat
  • Wildfires remove insulating vegetation
  • Buildings and roads trap and transfer heat downward
  • Poor drainage allows warm water to pool and thaw the ground

What is Erosion?

Erosion is the gradual wearing away and movement of soil, rock, or coastline material by wind, water, and ice. In the North, permafrost acts like “frozen glue” that holds the ground together. When permafrost weakens or melts, the land becomes loose and erosion accelerates rapidly.

What is Permafrost Thawing?

Permafrost thawing happens when once-permanently frozen ground warms and begins to melt. As the ice inside the soil turns to water, the land can slump, sink, or wash away, putting northern communities and coastlines at risk.

How does Permafrost relate to Erosion?

Permafrost acts like a frozen foundation that locks sediments in place and keeps the ground stable. When it begins to thaw, the soil loses structure and becomes soft and unstable, allowing wind and water to remove material much more easily.
